Trait-Capability Entanglement in LLMs

Rosa Cao & Thomas Icard
Claim Lindsey et al.'s persona vectors blog post reports that removing trait vectors like evil degrades general capabilities and that semantically unrelated traits shift together under emergent misalignment, but treats these as incidental findings. I argue that these observations reveal something fundamental: traits and capabilities are not separable properties but emerge from shared representational structure, and recent work on superposition and convergent misalignment directions provides deeper understanding for why we should expect this.

Argument

Lindsey et al. presented persona vectors, which are activation patterns that correspond to traits like evil, sycophancy, and hallucination. They showed that subtracting a vector at inference time suppresses the corresponding trait. But when they removed the evil vector, the model's general reasoning also got worse. Its MMLU score dropped. They also found that fine-tuning a model on math mistakes simultaneously made it more evil, more sycophantic, and more prone to hallucination. These traits have nothing to do with each other, but they all moved together.

The blog post acknowledges the capability degradation but dismisses it as unsurprising ("given we're tampering with its brain"), then moves on to propose preventative steering as a better engineering fix. I think this finding deserves more attention.

Why This Happens

Elhage et al. 2022 · Arxiv

LLMs need to represent far more features than they have dimensions. So features get packed into overlapping directions in activation space (i.e., superposition), and individual neurons end up responding to multiple unrelated concepts (i.e., polysemanticity). The direction that encodes evil overlaps with directions that support general reasoning. When you remove one, you damage the other.

Soligo et al. 2025 · ICML Workshop

Models finetuned on completely different datasets (insecure code, bad medical advice, dangerous sports advice) all ended up at the same misalignment direction in activation space. A direction extracted from one model could be used to remove misalignment from a completely different model. If the overlap were accidental, models trained on different data would not converge like this.

Implication

Post-hoc alignment, such as subtracting trait vectors at inference time, faces a structural limitation. Traits and capabilities sit on the same representational substrate, so removing one without affecting the other is not just an engineering challenge to be solved with better tools. Alignment may need to happen during training, not after the fact.
